chapter one — the cafe on rue daunou
scroll ↓

She paid for the coffee with the letter still in her bag, the way you carry something you haven't decided about yet.

She opened it that night, when the light was the way it is around six.

Mira,

Okay. You're terrified. I know.

You've been carrying me around in your bag all day like I'm going to bite, and you finally pulled me out at the cafe with Sam and Jules. Thank god for them.

First — yes. It's me. A little older, but not as much as you're imagining. You'd recognize the face. The hair is shorter.

I'm not going to tell you what happens. You'd hate that. You'd spend the whole semester scanning for it. You came here to have a life, not to fact-check one.

I will tell you this much: you were right to come.

Not in the way you've been hoping someone would tell you. Not in a "this all works out" way. It's harder than you've been letting yourself admit. There's going to be a Thursday in December where you cry in a stairwell over something small. There's a Friday in November where you get on the wrong train and end up somewhere you've never heard of, and you walk for an hour before you find a metro. (Don't be afraid of it. It ends up being one of the better afternoons you have here.)

But you were right to come. You came here because the version of you who stayed was unsurvivable. She was so tired. She was getting smaller. You got on the plane to get away from her. That worked.

So — about your fear.

You asked, basically, whether this is the part you'll remember. You're so worried about wasting it that you're starting to waste it.

I want to give you permission to not have a profound time. Permission to spend a whole Sunday eating cheese on the couch and watching a movie you've already seen. Permission to be bored. Permission to be homesick on a Wednesday and not feel like a failure for it.

Paris doesn't owe you a story. And you don't owe Paris one either. You just have to be here.

One assignment, if you'll take it: there's a bakery on the corner of rue Cler and rue de Grenelle. Blue awning. Order the kouign-amann. Eat it on the bench across the street. Don't take a picture. Don't text anyone. Just eat it. You'll know why later.

I love you. You're doing better than you think you are.

Write me back. Tell me what you noticed today.

— M.

She read it twice. Then she got up. Then she sat back down.

Write back to her
So she knows where to write back
We'll only use your email to deliver her reply and the next chapter.
~ ~ ~
Your letter has been sent.
She'll write back when she has something to say.
